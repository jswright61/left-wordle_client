Passkeys & Email

How account login works in Left Wordle, and what stays anonymous.

1.What is a passkey?

A passkey is a replacement for a username and password. When you create one, your device (or a password manager like iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager, or 1Password) generates a pair of cryptographic keys. The private key never leaves that secure store — it can't be typed, copied out, or intercepted. Only the matching public key is sent to the server.

To log in, the server sends a challenge, and your device signs it with the private key, unlocked by whatever you already use to unlock your device: Face ID, Touch ID, a PIN, or a security key. The signature proves you hold the private key without ever transmitting a secret over the network, which is why passkeys can't be phished, guessed, or leaked in a server data breach the way passwords can.

Passkeys are also scoped to the exact site that created them, so a phishing site with a lookalike domain simply can't request yours. And because most passkey managers sync passkeys across your devices (the same way they sync passwords today), a single passkey created on one device is often already usable on your other devices without any extra setup.

2.How Left Wordle uses passkeys

Left Wordle has no usernames and no passwords anywhere. When you create a passkey, it's registered as a discoverable (resident) credential, which means your device can present it without the server ever asking "who are you?" first. Logging in is a single tap: the browser asks your passkey manager for any passkey that matches this site, you approve it, and you're in. There's no field to type an identifier into at all.

The use of a password manager can, assuming the password manager is on both devices, allow you to login from a browser on your desktop pc and your phone. The passkey lives in the password manager which exercises control over its use (requires biometric aythentication) and syncs it securely to the devices on which you have this password manager.

3.Complete anonymity by default

Creating an account requires nothing but a passkey. No email, name, phone number, or any other identifying detail is collected unless you explicitly choose to add one. Under the hood, your account is just a randomly generated ID with zero information about who you are — the email column on an account is empty unless you fill it in.

Because login is usernameless, the server never even needs to look up "which account is this?" before authenticating you — your unique passkey or possibly multiple unique passkeys (see emailing links below) is tied to your account - similar to the way a user name and password identify your account on other systems.

4.Adding a device without an email

Passkey sync only works between devices that share the same passkey manager account. If you want to add a device that isn't signed into that same manager — say, a friend's phone, a work laptop, or a password manager that doesn't sync passkeys — "Add a Device (Link)" in the account menu generates a one-time, single-use link tied to your account. Opening that link on the new device and completing a short passkey registration adds a brand-new passkey to your existing (still anonymous) account. The link expires quickly and stops working the moment it's used once, so it's safe to hand off manually — no email required. Once registered with a passkey, that device will be able to log in anytime until that passkey is revoked. Either by whatever is controlling the passkey on the device, or by managing your Passkeys from a logged in session on this site.

5.What adding an email buys you

Setting an email doesn't change how you log in — logging in is always a passkey, never a password or a magic link. What an email does is give the same one-time device-link a second, more convenient delivery path: instead of manually copying or transferring the link yourself, "Email Me a Link" sends it straight to your inbox, ready to open on the new device. It also allows you to recconnect to your online left Wordle account should you lose access to all of your devices with Passkeys for this site.

6.The trade-off

An email address is the one piece of real-world identity Left Wordle can associate with your play history and stats, so adding one is a deliberate, permanent-until-you-remove-it trade of some anonymity for convenience. It's entirely optional, and can be added, changed, or left blank at any time from the account menu.

Worth noting
Access to your email inbox alone can lead to access to your account. An emailed link lets you begin registering a new passkey on whatever device opens it — anyone with access to your email could create a Passkey on a device they control which would have access to your account.

If you'd rather keep the account fully anonymous, that's the default — simply never set an email, and use "Add a Device (Link)" or use your trusted password manager to bring new devices onto the account instead.
